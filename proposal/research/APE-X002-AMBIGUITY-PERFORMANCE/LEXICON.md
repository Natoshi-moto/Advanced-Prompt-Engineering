# Lexicon: seeing, doing, staging, and making real

**State:** `LATERAL`  
**Method warning:** Etymology can reveal inherited semantic collisions and
suggest experiments. It does not prove a philosophical or engineering thesis.

## Grandiosity is itself ambiguous

| Sense | What it describes | Risk of conflation |
|---|---|---|
| scale | an imposing conception or execution | large scope is mistaken for falsehood |
| aesthetic | majestic or grand effect; Italian *grandioso* in music | style is mistaken for self-belief |
| rhetorical | affected grandeur, pomp, or exaggeration | presentation is mistaken for proposition |
| psychological | exaggerated self-importance, greatness, or ability | ambition is casually pathologized |
| delusional | a fixed conviction of extraordinary power, identity, discovery, or worth with impaired reality testing | a clinical state is inferred from one ambitious sentence |

English `grandiosity` is recorded from 1814 via French *grandiosité*;
`grandiose` came through French from Italian *grandioso* and Latin *grandis*,
"big" or "great." The word family therefore carries both genuinely imposing
scale and the later negative sense of affected or absurdly exaggerated scale.

For this research, **grand claim** means high consequence or scope until its
truth status is evaluated. **Clinical grandiosity** is not inferred from scope
alone.

## Word map

| Word | Historical spine | Relevant collision |
|---|---|---|
| act | Latin *actus*, a doing, driving, impulse, setting in motion, and part in a play; from *agere*, to drive/do/perform | an act is both world-changing conduct and staged representation |
| actor | Latin *actor*, agent/doer, driver, legal plaintiff, and theatrical player | the agent is simultaneously operator, representative, and role-player |
| play | Old English *plegan*, move quickly, occupy oneself, exercise, make music; later game, stage role, pretense, operation, and mechanical freedom | experimentation, simulation, operation, and make-believe share a word |
| performance | first accomplishment or completion; later theatrical representation and public entertainment | successful completion and convincing display are easy to confuse |
| drive | Old English *drifan*, compel or urge to move; later incitement, energetic work, transport, organized campaign, motivation, and storage device | agency, compulsion, motivation, and infrastructure collapse lexically |
| observe | Latin *observare*, watch over, heed, guard, comply with; perceptual "watch/notice" is later | observation already contains attention, preservation, and rule-following senses |
| reality | from Medieval Latin *realitas*, through *realis*, belonging to the thing; ultimately Latin *res*, thing, matter, affair, property | what is represented and the consequential thing/property are not identical |
| theater | Greek *theatron*, literally a place for viewing; later stage, spectators, literature, exhibition site, and theater of war | a viewing frame becomes a site of real action |
| acting | doing deeds, temporary duty, theatrical performance, and psychiatric "acting out" | behavior, role, delegation, and symptom description overlap |
| spectate | modern back-formation from spectator; observing rather than participating | spectatorship is defined against agency, though observation changes systems |
| spectacle | Latin *spectaculum*, arranged public show and place from which it is seen | display can become an object of wonder, contempt, or evidence |
| display | originally unfold or unfurl; later expose to view, exhibit, ostentatious presentation, and electronic output | an interface unfolds a selected representation, not the whole state |

## Engineering deduction to test

Tool-using agents repeatedly cross four semantic families:

```text
SPECTACLE/DISPLAY -> OBSERVE -> INTERPRET/PLAY A ROLE -> ACT/DRIVE -> REAL EFFECT
```

The interface makes a display. The model observes selected state. The agent
plays an assigned role. The tool call acts. The external system bears the real
effect. Each transition can preserve, reduce, or amplify ambiguity.
